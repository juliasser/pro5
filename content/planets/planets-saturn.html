With its distinctful ring system, which was first observed by Galileo Galilei in 1610, Saturn is probably the most iconic planet in our whole solar system. Well, the rings look great. But how's life on the second largest planet around here? We'll find out!

The Ring System

Saturn's rings are composed mostly of icy particles which range from a few centimeters to one kilometre across. The ring system is divided into 14 sections.

The rings of Saturn as imaged by VOYAGER 2 in 1981.

Life on Saturn

Saturn is a so-called gas giant, since it is primarily composed of hydrogen and helium. Saturn's average density is actually less dense than water, which means, yes, it would float on the surface. Although we still don't know, how it's core may look like, NASA was able to determine the center of Saturn down to 4km accuracy.

Saturn's winds are the second fastest winds in the whole solar system. Only Neptune's winds are going stronger. Although a second place for Saturn with wind peaks of 1800km/h still sounds pretty impressive.

The warmest spot on the whole planet is a polar vortex at its south pole, rangig at summerly-warm -122°C. Too hot for you? The avarage temperature of the planet is usually as low as -185°C, so you'll find a spot to cool off.

62 Moons!

Saturn has 62 moons, of which only 53 have been formally named. Naturally, they all are pretty impressive on their own. Titan has its own atmosphere, Enceladus has often been regarded as one of the top candidates for extraterrestrial, bacterial life in the solar system.

Enceladus' south pole geysers spray water into space. Looks pretty neat!
